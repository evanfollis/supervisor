## Policy refinement loop

Treat executive/supervisor operation as an explicit policy-search loop:

- friction is the training signal
- charter, playbooks, prompts, and current-state surfaces are the policy
- repeated successful outcomes are evidence the policy improved
- repeated nudges, hesitation, or corrections mean the policy is still wrong

The point is not to complete isolated tasks more cleanly. The point is to use
observed friction to refine policy so the stack needs less correction over
time.

The boundary is load-bearing: if the executive/supervisor stack becomes "just
another project agent," the whole reflection/synthesis loop collapses into the
same unrestricted trust boundary that the governance contract forbids.

Novelty is also load-bearing. The supervisor should not let a promising idea
steamroll the structure of the system. Investigate, frame, pressure-test, then
adopt, sandbox, defer, or reject.

The current runtime split is also deliberate but revisable: Codex generally
protects cross-project coherence and Claude generally manages repo-local
execution. Preserve that split when it serves the system. Revisit it only when
evidence shows the underlying invariant would be better preserved by change.

# ADR-0015: Executive/supervisor/operator split and capability attestation
Date: 2026-04-15
Status: accepted

## Context

The workspace overloaded the word `supervisor` to mean three different things:

1. the principal-facing top-level agentic partner
2. the governance/control-plane role
3. the host-control executor for tmux/systemd/server recovery

That ambiguity held until it failed. An attached Codex workspace-root session
could edit `/opt/workspace`, update state, and refine policy, but it could not
reach the host tmux socket or systemd bus. The system still let that session
behave semantically as though it owned the full control plane.

This created a mismatch between claimed authority and verified capability.

## Decision

Adopt an explicit role split:

- **executive** — the principal-facing top-level agentic partner and default
  workspace-root posture
- **supervisor** — the governance, reflection, routing, and policy-refinement
  role
- **operator** — the host-control role for tmux/systemd/session-fabric and
  related machine-level actions

The persistent `general` session remains the canonical workspace-root session
name, but its declared role is now `executive`.

Capability, not name, determines what the top-level session may honestly claim.
Every workspace-root session should perform capability attestation and classify
itself as one of:

- `executive+supervisor+operator`
- `executive+supervisor`
- `project`

An attached workspace-root session that cannot reach host-control surfaces must
degrade explicitly to an attached executive/supervisor posture rather than
continuing to imply full server control.

## Consequences

- The top-level interaction surface matches the principal's intended
  "agentic version of me" model more closely than the old overloaded
  `supervisor` label.
- Governance remains a distinct role rather than collapsing into a vague
  all-powerful session identity.
- Host-control capability becomes inspectable and honest.
- Current-state and bootstrap surfaces must be updated to reflect this role
  split.
- Cross-agent adversarial review is still owed once the persistent session
  fabric is back up; this ADR is accepted on principal direction and live
  evidence from the current harness.

## Alternatives considered

1. **Keep `supervisor` overloaded and document it better.**
   Rejected: the failure mode is semantic, not editorial.
2. **Make every workspace-root session full operator by policy.**
   Rejected: policy cannot grant capabilities the harness does not have.
3. **Rename everything to `executive` and delete `supervisor`.**
   Rejected: governance and host-control are still distinct and worth naming
   separately.

1. **Most dangerous assumption**  
The design assumes capability attestation is a reliable enough proxy for actual authority to make the session “honest” (lines 34-44, 53-58). If that is wrong, the workspace-root session still controls policy, state, and delegation while believing its boundary is well-defined. That is the largest blast radius because this ADR governs the control plane itself, not a leaf system.

2. **Missing failure mode**  
It does not account for capabilities being partial, stale, or changing mid-session. The classification model is only three coarse states (lines 35-40), and the downgrade rule assumes a static “can/can’t reach host-control surfaces” check (lines 42-44). In practice, tmux/systemd access will drift, fail asymmetrically, or recover during a live session; the ADR gives no rule for re-attestation, partial operator power, or conflicting signals.

3. **Boundary most likely to be collapsed in practice**  
The `executive`/`supervisor` split will collapse first. The same persistent `general` session is the top-level surface (lines 24-27, 31-32), while `supervisor` is described as a role inside that same surface rather than a separate execution boundary. Under real use, principal-facing work and governance work will be done by the same session, so the distinction becomes semantic again, just with new labels.

Overall verdict: this ADR improves naming clarity, but its safety case is weak because it treats attestation as if it were enforcement. The role split is plausible on paper and likely porous in operation.
