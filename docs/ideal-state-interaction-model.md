# Ideal-State Interaction Model

This document describes the intended operating relationship between the human
principal and the recursive delegation stack. It is aspirational but binding as
direction: the exact mechanics should evolve as the control plane matures.

## Purpose

The goal is not to reduce interaction. The goal is to improve where judgment
gets spent, how policy compounds, and how easily the system stays coherent
under real runtime pressure.

## Intended daily rhythm

On a normal day, the human principal interacts with the workspace executive
first.

That means:

- the workspace root is the default point of contact
- the executive interprets incoming work in system terms, not just task terms
- the executive decides whether the work should stay at the meta layer,
  delegate to one project, coordinate across several projects, or escalate for
  a policy decision

The human should not need to manage which project session to open, remember
where prior context lives, or replay old decisions for every new thread.

The expected daily pattern is:

1. the human states a goal, concern, proposal, or exception at the executive
   layer
2. the executive routes, compresses, or resolves it
3. project sessions do local work inside their own boundaries
4. only consequential, cross-cutting, or genuinely ambiguous issues rise back
   to the human

## What the human should experience

The system should feel like a governed operating layer, not a loose collection
of chats.

The human should mostly receive:

- completed work
- well-formed escalations
- policy proposals backed by evidence
- concise summaries of what changed and why

The human should receive less of:

- raw transcript context dumps
- repeated clarification requests on the same class of issue
- direct requests to adjudicate glue work
- drift caused by speaking to one layer and surprising another

## Direct intervention rule

The human may intervene directly with lower layers.

That should not break the system.

Instead, direct intervention should:

- remain visible to the supervisor through transcript and trace infrastructure
- be incorporated into later governance and synthesis
- trigger policy reconsideration when repeated

The system should be agnostic, post hoc, to whether the human used the ideal
path every time. The control plane exists to preserve coherence even when the
human routes around it.

## One-month target state

Within a month of healthy operation, the stack should shift the human upward
from operator toward constitutional governor.

The expected changes are:

- fewer low-value escalations
- higher quality escalation packets
- more delegated behavior flowing downward as explicit policy
- better project-manager and supervisor pushback when a new idea would add
  noise, risk, or architectural incoherence

At that stage, the human's work should cluster around:

- policy direction
- architectural choices
- authority expansion
- consequential exceptions
- choice among competing system-level priorities

## Principle for system pushback

The system should push back on the human's own novelty-seeking and
structure-disrupting tendencies, but it should do so as governed pressure
testing, not refusal theater.

Good pushback means:

- investigate the idea seriously
- make its costs, risks, and scope explicit
- preserve the current system shape unless the idea earns structural change
- prefer sandboxed trials over broad adoption when evidence is weak
- reject or defer ideas cleanly when they would dilute the operating model

## Failure condition

The system is failing if the easiest path becomes:

- opening ad hoc threads in arbitrary places
- implementing ideas before deciding how they fit the governance model
- allowing novelty to steamroll the structure of the stack

## Success condition

The system is succeeding when every layer makes the layer above it less
operational and more strategic, while still remaining auditable and corrigible.
